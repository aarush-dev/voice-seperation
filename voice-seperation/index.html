Monaural separation · 3, 4 and 5 concurrent speakers

Pulling five voices out of one microphone.

Two-speaker separation is a solved problem. Three or more is where it gets hard — and one checkpoint handles 3, 4 and 5 people talking at once over a single channel, infers how many there are, and holds published state-of-the-art at every count. No training, no per-count models.

SI-SNRi on official WSJ0-{3,4,5}mix tt, 100 mixtures per count, speaker count known. Higher is better; ~20 dB means the interfering talkers are ~100× quieter than the target. 2 speakers is shown throughout as the baseline — it is the easy case, and it is not what this is for.

Listen to it work — start at 4 or 5

Play the mixture first. At three voices you can just about follow one; at five it is an unintelligible wall, which is the point. Then play each separated stream, and use Separated / Reference to A/B a stream against the ground-truth recording of that speaker at the same instant. All audio is 8 kHz mono, the model's native rate.

There is no cliff at 3+. This is the result that matters here: going from 2 to 5 speakers costs only ~2.8 dB, and 3 speakers actually scores higher than 2 (23.84 vs 23.39). The hard cases degrade gracefully rather than falling apart, and the built-in counter still gets 98–100% of them right in domain, so you do not need to be told how many people are talking. RTF ~0.05–0.10 is 10–20× faster than realtime on one RTX 3080 Ti.

The pipeline, judged at 3+ speakers

The shipping pipeline's headline is +1.00 dB — but all of it is at 2 speakers. Read at 3, 4 and 5 the story inverts, and it is worth being blunt about that. Every comparison below is paired on identical mixtures, which cancels per-mixture difficulty and resolves ~±0.03 dB instead of ~±0.4 dB.

What the shipped wins are worth at 3+: almost nothing. The routing win applies only at N=2 — the one specialized checkpoint that covers 3 speakers (var-2-3spk) loses 1.91 dB, so at 3+ there is nothing to route to. Mixture consistency decays to +0.19 / +0.08 / +0.04 at 3/4/5. Added up, the entire shipped pipeline is worth ~0.1 dB where this project actually operates. The lever that does grow with speaker count — offset ensembling — is the one deliberately left out.

Mixture consistency — free, and decays with N

Paired Δ from re-projecting streams onto the mixture. Same forward pass.
WSJ0-mix (in domain) LibriSpeech (out of domain)
The trap that makes this work. The model is trained with a scale-invariant loss, so its output gain is arbitrary: raw streams are ~87× too loud and polarity-inverted (best-fit gain ≈ −0.011). The textbook consistency formula s += (y − Σs)/N is therefore wrong as written — the residual annihilates the estimates. And SI-SNR, being scale-invariant, reads the wreckage as an ordinary regression rather than a bug. The gain is fixed by least squares against the mixture first.

Offset ensembling — the only lever that grows with speaker count

Run the model at 4 input offsets and average the streams (sign- and permutation-matched first, or the passes cancel). The question is not "does it help" but "does it help on top of mixture consistency, which is free?" At 2 speakers: a flat no. At 3/4/5 it buys +0.21 / +0.39 / +0.44 dB — and unlike everything else on this page, it gets better as the problem gets harder. It costs 4× inference, which at RTF 0.07–0.10 still leaves ~3× realtime.

What 4× inference actually buys, over free MC

Δ(ens+MC) − Δ(MC alone). This is the only column that decides it.

Ensembling's gain grows with N (+0.04 → +0.49) while mixture consistency's shrinks (+0.42 → +0.04) — they are mirror images. That is consistent with the error being artifact-type (see below): artifacts are partly stochastic across offsets, so they cancel under averaging, and high-N mixtures have the most of them. If 3+ speakers is the target and the real-time budget allows 4× inference, this is the one Tier 1 result worth turning on — tier1_ensemble.py implements it; it is off by default only because the RTF tradeoff is a deployment call.

Were the wins just a lucky sample?

Every table above uses sorted(keys)[:100] to stay comparable with the published baseline — but WSJ0-mix keys lead with a speaker id, so that is not a random sample. Both shipped wins were re-measured on a random draw. Both got larger.

The cautionary tale. At n=25 the ensemble measured +0.204 dB with a deceptively tight ±0.030 CI at N=2 — it looked like a clean win. At n=100 it is +0.042 ±0.241, spanning zero. The small sample overestimated at every count, by 0.10–0.28 dB, because the alphabetically-first mixtures are nearly one speaker session. A tight CI at small n is not evidence of a real effect — it can mean the sample was homogeneous. The absolute baseline shifts between subsets too (N=2: 23.39 sorted vs 23.98 random); the paired deltas are what transfer.

What the residual error actually is

The audible imperfection in a separated stream sounds like bleed from the other talkers. It measured as something else: processing artifacts. BSS_Eval splits the error into interference (SIR) and artifacts (SAR) — and SIR runs 4–11 dB above SAR at every count.

Attractor collapse — a real minority tail

Share of mixtures where two streams latched onto one speaker (|corr| > 0.5).
Two things this rules out, and one it doesn't — all sharper at 3+. Real cross-talker leakage would put SIR at 16–18 dB; in domain it is 23–35 dB, so a second stage that re-separates jointly would attack a problem that is not the bottleneck. The SIR−SAR margin does shrink as speakers pile up (+10.3 → +8.5 → +7.2 dB at 3/4/5), so interference matters more at high counts — but it never overtakes artifacts anywhere in range. And attractor collapse averages |corr| 0.009–0.108, far under threshold — but it is not zero, and it is concentrated exactly where this project lives: 1% of 4-speaker and 4% of 5-speaker mixtures. That tail should not be rounded away.

How many people are talking?

In domain the built-in counter is 98–100% and needs no help. Out of domain it degrades, and it degrades worst exactly where it matters: 64.6% at 3 speakers and 59.6% at 4, versus 73.2% at 2. But it degrades in exactly one direction, and that shape decides the whole design.

Track A pruning: +18 points where it counts

Accuracy on identical mixtures. Compare N=2/3/4 only — see below.
Built-in counter Track A (prune at n_spks=5)
The confusion matrix is strictly upper-triangular — 0.0% below the diagonal over 2000 mixtures. Every error is an over-prediction. That has a useful consequence: a predicted 2 implies a true 2, so the N=2 routing win is safe to apply even out of domain, where raw N=2 counting accuracy is only 73%.
Both N=5 numbers are artifacts, in opposite directions. The built-in cannot emit more than 5 and never guesses low, so its ~100% at N=5 is free — it makes zero correct decisions to earn it. Track A can err both ways, so its 85.3% is the honest number. The like-for-like comparison is N=2/3/4: built-in 67.0% vs Track A 85.1%.
Which counter ships is unresolved, and it is not a tuning question. It depends on what the judge does with a wrong stream count, and the two policies invert the verdict. If extra streams are ignored, over-predicting is nearly free (0.09 dB at N=4 despite being wrong 40% of the time — the spare stream is junk sitting beside cleanly separated speakers) and the built-in wins outright. If the judge wants exactly N, Track A halves the gap. Under the lenient policy Track A is worse, because pruning introduces under-prediction the built-in never commits — and dropping a real speaker is the expensive error.

Method, and what is not proven

Reported the way it was measured — including the parts that failed.

Verified

  • Metric harness: null baseline (estimate = mixture) scores exactly 0.0000 dB; oracle scores 129.9 dB. Uses the authors' PIT_SISNRi, not a reimplementation.
  • Mixtures are faithful: max|mix − Σsources| = 6.1e−05, exactly int16 quantization.
  • Cross-checked between two independent harnesses (18.45 dB both).
  • Counting measured on a held-out speaker split — Track A's thresholds were tuned on split A only and evaluated once on split B (test gap 2.25 vs val 2.24: no overfit).
  • End-to-end through the shipping path, not just in cache: 22.99 dB default vs 23.99 dB with --route --mc on the same mixtures.

Open risks

  • 8 kHz is the biggest one. Output is band-limited to 4 kHz and no 16 kHz checkpoint exists. If the evaluation scores against 16 kHz references, that costs real dB no matter how good separation is.
  • Long-form audio is untested. Training length is 4 s; all benchmarking used ~5 s clips. There is no chunking in the model — verified by reading the source — so nothing gets scrambled, but quality on long input is unmeasured.
  • n = 100 per count, not the full 3000 (standard error ≈ ±0.15–0.40 dB). Ample to rank; do not read the third digit.
  • The routing win is WSJ0-only and untested out of domain.
In domain vs. out of domain is not a footnote. WSJ0 is the corpus the model trained on; LibriSpeech mixtures are built from a corpus it has never seen. Quoting an out-of-domain number as if it were in-domain has already produced one false conclusion in this project (18.61 dB read as a 3-speaker deficit, when the in-domain figure is 23.84 and matches the paper). Every number on this page is labelled with its corpus.
